Extracto
As a Basque girl growing in a Spanish-speaking family in Bilbao,
I know firsthand the difficulties of managing conflicting identities
and cultures, especially when one of those identities is a minority
in society. My parents, who do not speak Basque as a result of
the repression during the dictatorship, understood the importance
of introducing me to the Basque language at an early age, so I
was able to study at an Ikastola (Basque School). Unlike most of
my classmates, my contact with the language came only from the
institutionalized curriculum. Thus, I have always felt at a liminal
position regarding my identities; never quite Basque, but not
completely Spanish either. As a result, questions of belonging have
always been in the forefront of my interests as a scholar. Now, as
an immigrant doctor of Spanish literature and language in the United
States, both my research and my teaching are infused with those
preoccupations about identity. As such, I strive to include as many
different voices and experiences in my classroom as possible. I am
aware of acknowledging the importance of the malleability of identity
to foster diversity in the classroom.
